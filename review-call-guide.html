Review Call · Phase 2
Portfolio Snapshot & Health Scorecard
Minutes 5 – 15
Objective
Walk through the portfolio snapshot and health scorecard so the client sees their entire portfolio summarized mathematically for the first time. The red scores on the scorecard do the emotional work — let them land without rushing past them.
Do
Share your screen and open Page 1 of the diagnostic document — the Portfolio Snapshot. Confirm the numbers with them briefly.
"Page 1 shows your complete portfolio snapshot. You have [X] properties across [Y] markets, total value approximately [Z], annual NOI [amount], leverage ratio [%]. Does that match your understanding? Good — let us move to what these numbers actually mean."
Do
Move to Page 2 — the Health Scorecard. Walk through each metric slowly. Pause on every red score. Do not rush this page — it creates the most impact of any section in the report.
"Page 2 is your Portfolio Health Scorecard. Every metric in your portfolio rated across 12 dimensions. Green means healthy. Amber means monitor. Red means this needs attention. Let me walk you through what each one means for your specific situation."
Note
For every red metric — state the specific number, explain what it means in plain English, and state what the target should be. Never just say "this is red." Always quantify.
"Your Sharpe ratio is 0.54. That means you are earning 54 cents of return for every unit of risk you are taking. An optimized equivalent portfolio should produce between 0.85 and 1.10. You are taking the right amount of risk — you are just not being compensated for it properly. That is a structural problem in how your holdings are weighted, and it is completely fixable."
Ask
After the scorecard, pause and ask a specific question to gauge their reaction and invite engagement.
"Before we go deeper — which of these metrics surprised you the most? What were you expecting to see that you are not seeing here?"

Review Call · Phase 3
The 3 Biggest Portfolio Problems
Minutes 15 – 30
Objective
Walk through each of the 3 biggest mathematical problems with specific numbers. This is the most emotionally impactful section of the call. Speak slowly. Let the numbers land. Do not soften them — the client paid for honesty.
Critical Rule
Never say "I think." Always say "The math shows." Every problem must be stated with a specific number attached. A problem without a number is an opinion. A problem with a number is a diagnosis.
Say
Introduce the section and frame the 3 problems as the core finding of the entire engagement.
"Page 3 is the most important page in your report. Based on the full analysis I identified 3 mathematical problems in your portfolio — each one costing you return, increasing your risk, or both. I am going to walk through each one carefully. Please stop me at any point if you want to go deeper on anything."
Do
Present Problem 1. State the problem title. Read the plain English description. Then state the three specific metrics — current value, benchmark, and cost. Pause after each number.
"Problem 1 is [title]. Here is what the math shows: [describe]. Your current [metric] is [number]. The target is [benchmark]. This is costing your portfolio approximately [annual dollar impact] per year. This is your most critical issue."
Ask
After Problem 1 — before moving to Problem 2 — ask a clarifying question to confirm understanding and invite their response.
"Does that make sense? Were you aware this was happening in your portfolio?"
Do
Present Problem 2 using the same structure — title, plain English description, three specific numbers, annual cost.
Do
Present Problem 3. For near-term risk problems like an expiring variable rate loan — state the specific timeline and what happens if nothing is done.
"Problem 3 is time-sensitive. You have [amount] of variable rate debt maturing in [date]. Under a [X] basis point rate increase your DSCR on that property drops to [number] — below the 1.0 minimum threshold. You have [X] months to act before the refinancing window narrows. This is your most urgent priority."
Ask
After all 3 problems — pause and ask the single most important question of the call.
"Of the three problems we just covered — which one concerns you the most right now? And did any of these surprise you or confirm something you already suspected?"
Notes — client response to the 3 problems

Review Call · Phase 4
Efficient Frontier Gap
Minutes 30 – 38
Objective
Show the client exactly where their portfolio sits versus where it could sit on the efficient frontier. The annual dollar gap is the single most powerful number in the entire report. Present it clearly and let it breathe.
Say
Introduce the efficient frontier concept briefly before showing the chart. Keep it simple — one or two sentences maximum.
"Page 5 shows the efficient frontier — the curve representing the maximum possible return for every level of risk. Every dot on this curve is an optimal portfolio. Your portfolio is the red dot. The green dot is where an optimized version of your portfolio sits — at exactly the same risk level you are already taking."
Do
Point to the chart. Show the gap between red dot and green dot visually. Then immediately state the dollar figure — do not make them calculate it themselves.
"Your current portfolio returns [X]% annually at a Sharpe ratio of [X]. The optimized portfolio returns [Y]% at the same risk level — a Sharpe ratio of [Y]. That gap in annual return on your [portfolio value] portfolio is [dollar amount] per year. Over a 5-year hold that is [5-year figure]. At the same level of risk you are already taking."
Note
Pause after the dollar figure. Do not speak immediately. Let the number land. Give them 5–10 seconds of silence. This is intentional — silence after a significant number creates more impact than any follow-up sentence.
Ask
After the silence — ask one direct question.
"What does that number mean to you in the context of your overall financial goals?"
Notes — client reaction to the dollar gap

Review Call · Phase 5
Monte Carlo Stress Testing
Minutes 38 – 45
Objective
Show the client the full range of possible outcomes across 10,000 simulations and 6 economic scenarios. Focus on the downside scenarios — that is what creates urgency. Most clients have never seen a probability distribution of their own returns before.
Say
Introduce the Monte Carlo section with a brief explanation of what 10,000 simulations means in plain language.
"Page 4 shows your Monte Carlo stress test. I ran 10,000 different scenarios for your portfolio — varying rent growth, vacancy rates, interest rates, and exit conditions across thousands of combinations. The bar chart shows the distribution of all those outcomes. Let me walk you through what your portfolio looks like under each scenario."
Do
Walk through each scenario card — best case, median, worst case, recession, rate spike. State the IRR for each. Focus most time on the recession and rate spike scenarios.
"In the best 10% of scenarios your portfolio returns [X]%. In the median scenario — the most likely outcome — [Y]%. In the worst 10% of scenarios [Z]%. Now the recession scenario — GDP contraction, 15% vacancy increase, falling rents — your portfolio produces [W]% IRR. This is where your correlation problem becomes most visible. Because your properties move together, a single market downturn hits the whole portfolio simultaneously."
Say
Connect the Monte Carlo findings back to the correlation problem from Section 3. Show them how the problems are linked.
"The reason your downside scenarios look like this is directly connected to Problem 1 we discussed — the high correlation between your holdings. An optimized portfolio with lower correlation would produce a much wider and more favorable distribution. Higher median returns and significantly less catastrophic downside."
Ask
Check their understanding and reaction.
"Looking at these scenarios — which one feels most relevant to what you think could actually happen in the markets you are in? And does the downside scenario change how you think about your current structure?"
Notes — Monte Carlo reactions

Review Call · Phase 6
Optimization Recommendations
Minutes 45 – 52
Objective
Present each recommendation with its mathematical justification. Never present a recommendation as an opinion. Every action must be tied to a specific improvement in Sharpe ratio, drawdown, or return. This section turns diagnosis into direction.
Say
Frame the recommendations as solutions to the 3 problems — connecting back to what they already heard.
"Now that we have seen the problems clearly — here is exactly what needs to change and what each change does to your numbers. Every recommendation is prioritized and mathematically justified. I am not telling you what to do — I am showing you what the math says will happen if you do."
Do
Present each recommendation using the exact same format every time: action → mathematical impact → timeline.
"Recommendation 1: [action]. If you do this, your Sharpe ratio moves from [X] to [Y] and your maximum drawdown reduces by [Z]%. Timeline: [when to execute]. Recommendation 2: [action]. This moves your portfolio from [current position] to [improved position] on the efficient frontier, adding approximately [dollar amount] in annual return."
Note
If the client pushes back on a recommendation — do not defend it emotionally. Return to the numbers.
"I understand the hesitation. Let me show you exactly what happens to your Sharpe ratio and drawdown if we keep this property versus removing it. The numbers make the case better than I can."
Ask
After all recommendations — invite their overall reaction.
"Looking at these recommendations as a set — do they feel directionally right based on what you know about your portfolio? Is there anything here you want to discuss further or that you have specific concerns about?"
Notes — recommendation reactions and pushback

Review Call · Phase 7
Implementation Roadmap & Dashboard
Minutes 52 – 57
Objective
Show the client the path forward — what to do, in what order, and when. Then introduce the monitoring dashboard and Python models so they understand what they will be operating independently after the handoff session.
Say
Frame the roadmap as the bridge between analysis and action.
"The implementation roadmap on Page 8 turns everything we just discussed into a specific sequence of actions. Not a general direction — specific steps, in priority order, with timelines and trigger conditions for each one."
Do
Walk through the first 3 priority actions on the roadmap — just the most urgent ones. Give the full timeline but spend most time on what needs to happen in the next 90 days.
"Your most urgent action — in the next 90 days — is [action]. Second priority over the next 3–6 months is [action]. Third priority over 6–12 months is [action]. The full 36-month roadmap is in your report but these first three are what matter most right now."
Say
Introduce the monitoring dashboard and Python models — explain what they are and that they will learn to use them in the handoff session.
"Along with your report you are also receiving your monitoring dashboard — an Excel template pre-loaded with your portfolio data that you update monthly — and 4 Python model files: your Monte Carlo simulator, correlation matrix, efficient frontier model, and Sharpe ratio calculator. All calibrated to your specific portfolio. We will go through how to use every one of these in your 60-minute handoff session."
Notes — roadmap discussion

Review Call · Phase 8
Questions & Revisions
Minutes 57 – 62
Objective
Answer all remaining questions. Collect specific revision requests. Confirm what changes need to be made to the report before final delivery. This phase turns the call into a collaborative refinement rather than a one-way presentation.
Ask
Open the Q&A with a broad invitation before asking about specific revisions.
"We have covered the full report. Before we wrap up — what questions do you have? Anything you want me to go deeper on, anything that needs more explanation, or anything in the analysis you disagree with?"
Note
If they disagree with a finding — do not defend immediately. Ask what they are seeing differently and why. Sometimes they have local knowledge your model did not capture. Listen first, then respond with the data.
"That is a fair challenge. Tell me more about what you are seeing there — is there something specific about that market or property that you think changes the analysis? I want to make sure the model reflects reality as accurately as possible."
Ask
Collect revision requests specifically.
"Are there any specific changes you want me to make to the report before I send you the final version? Different assumptions, additional scenarios, or anything you want me to add or adjust?"
Do
Write down every revision request clearly. Confirm each one back to them before ending the call so there is no ambiguity about what is changing.
"Let me confirm what I have: you want me to [revision 1], [revision 2], and [revision 3]. Is that everything? Good — I will incorporate those and send you the final report within [1–2 days]."
Revision requests — list every one clearly

Review Call · Phase 9
Closing & Next Steps
Minutes 62 – 65
Objective
Close the call cleanly. Confirm every next step with specific dates. Schedule the handoff session before ending. The client should leave the call knowing exactly what happens next and when — no ambiguity.
Say
Summarize the 3 key findings and the 3 most urgent actions in one short paragraph. This locks in the most important takeaways before they leave the call.
"Before we wrap up — let me summarize the three most important things from today. First: [biggest problem and its cost]. Second: [efficient frontier gap in dollars]. Third: [most urgent action and timeline]. These three things are the core of your optimization path."
Say
Confirm the next steps with specific timelines before the call ends.
"Here is exactly what happens next. I will incorporate your revisions and send you the final report within [1–2 days]. Along with the report you will receive your monitoring dashboard, your 4 Python model files, your decision framework document, and your implementation roadmap as separate files. Then we schedule your 60-minute handoff session — I would like to do that within the week. What does your schedule look like on [day] or [day]?"
Do
Schedule the handoff session before ending the call. Do not leave it as "I will send you a calendar invite." Book it now while you have them on the call.
Say
Close the call professionally and with confidence.
"This has been a thorough engagement and your portfolio is in a significantly better position for having gone through this process. The numbers are clear. The path is clear. I will have your final report to you by [date]. Your handoff session is confirmed for [date]. Talk then."
